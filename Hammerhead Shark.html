    • Hammerhead Shark

    Overview

    There are 10 hammerhead shark species and they are a type of shark part of the Sphyrnidae family. They are called “hammerhead” as their head is flattened laterally extended, in the shape of a hammer. The shape is called the cephalofoil. They can be as small as 2 feet (~60cm) or as big as 9 feet (~274cm). This shark also has 360° vision as their eyes are placed at the ends of their head but they cannot see in front of them. The average lifespan of a hammerhead shark is 20 to 30 years with the oldest recorded age to be around 40 years.

    Diet & Habitat

    Hammerhead sharks are mostly found in warm water near the coastlines and continental shelves. They are often seen migrating in my groups to cooler waters during the summer season. Hammerhead sharks are solitary and hunt prey individually during the night but swim in “schools” during the day. The sharks’ diet include octopi, squid, crustaceans and other smaller fish which may include other sharks. They do sometimes feed on seagrass and are considered potentially omnivorous. In addition, the great hammerhead occasionally engages in cannibalism, consuming other hammerhead sharks, including its own young.

    Threats & Diseases

    All hammerhead shark species are classified as endangered or critically endangered on the IUCN red list, with the exception of Smooth Hammerhead which is considered vulnerable. Unfortunately, the hammerhead shark is widely targeted by commercial fishing, mainly for the shark fin industry for the production of shark fin soup. In fact, the total number of hammerhead sharks landed in fisheries rose substantially from 75 metric tons in 1990 to 6,313 metric tons in 2010.
    Other common threats that hammerhead sharks face include:

    • Entanglement in fishing gear
    • Habitat destruction

    In addition to this, there are various infections, parasites, and bacterial diseases that can be obtained by hammerhead sharks, harming their health.

    Fun Facts!

    • Hammerhead sharks do not have any bones and are not possible to be turned into fossils.
    • Hammerhead sharks only reproduce once a year, and when they do, it usually involves the male biting the female aggressively until she consents to mate. They can also produce asexually.
    • Great hammerhead sharks can swim sideways.
