    • Seals

    Overview

    Seals, scientifically known as Pinnipedia (meaning fin-footed), are semi-aquatic marine mammals. There are 33 species of seals including sea lions, walruses, elephant seals, eared seals, and more. The average lifespan of a wild seal is 30 years with the oldest recorded seal to be 47 years.

    Diet & Habitat

    Seals are found on all continents, however, the majority are found on the coasts and cold waters of Antarctica and the Arctic. Seals are able to insulate themselves by their thick layer of blubber (fat) and dense fur (with the exception of Walruses). Moreover, seals are classified as carnivorous, with their diet relying on the consumption of fishes caught at sea. Despite this, some types of seals also have other diets. For example, leopard seals survive on a diet of penguins or other seals, and walruses make a living eating shellfish.

    Threats & Diseases

    Currently, the biggest threats to seals are global warming, fisheries, oil spillings, habitat loss, and hunting. According to the IUCN Red list, the following are the endangerment status of seals:

    • Extinct: Japanese Sea Lion and the Caribbean monk seal
    • Endangered: Galapagos fur seal, Hawaiian monk seal, Caspian seal, and Mediterranean monk seal
    • All other seals are classified as least concern or vulnerable

    In addition to these threats, there are also various diseases that seals can obtain. These include respiratory infections, parasitic infections, sealpox, and one of the deadliest viruses is the phocine distemper virus (aka the seal virus).

    Fun Facts:

    • Seals Are Related to Bears, Skunks, and Badgers
    • Seals use clicking or trilling noises to communicate
    • Male seals are called bulls; females are called cows; and babies are called pups.
