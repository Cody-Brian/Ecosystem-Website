    • Blue Whale

    Overview

    Blue whales, scientifically known as balaenoptera musculus, are a type of baleen whale and belong in the family, Balaenopteridae. They are the largest animal on Earth at around 28.9 metres (98ft) long and weigh up to 199,000kg (199 tonnes)! It is estimated that blue whales have an average lifespan of 80 to 90 years, with the oldest being 110 years. In addition, they can communicate with each other in 8 to 25 hertz.

    Diet and Habitat

    Blue whales are found in all oceans except the Arctic. The majority will migrate between the poles during the summer and the tropics during the winter. The diet of blue whales mainly consists of krill. When blue whales lunge feed, they swim towards the krill at great speeds while opening their mouths. They have the capacity to swallow 220 metric tons of water at once and force the water out of their baleen plates and ingest the remaining krill.

    Threats and Diseases

    Despite there being over 350,000 whales in the past, blue whales were almost hunted down to extinction towards the end of the 19th century. Now, the blue whale is categorised as endangered on the IUCN red list with an estimated population between 10,000 to 25,000. Fortunately, due to new regulation and conservation efforts, the population is slowly increasing. In addition, there are various infections, parasites, and bacterial diseases that can be obtained by blue whales.
    The common threats that blue whales are facing include:

    • Habitat loss and toxins
    • Fishing gear entanglement
    • Vessel strikes

    Fun Facts!

    • The tongue of the blue whale is as big as an elephant.
    • Blue whales are also the loudest animals on Earth.
    • Blue whale calves gain roughly 90-kilograms of weight per day in its first year of life.
